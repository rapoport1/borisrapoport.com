What I do

At WeSuite I’m head of QA and the team’s process engineer and agile coach. Practically: I’m the last sign off before any release ships, and I run the manual testing across our stack — SSMS for the database side, IIS for hosting our web apps, a long lived WinForms desktop app, and a newer web app. Test automation lives with the developers so I focus on the things automation misses, on prioritizing the bug backlog, and on the ceremonies (planning, standups, retrospectives, handovers) that keep the team moving. I’m the bridge between development and sales, marketing, professional services, and support.

A recent project

This past year we shipped a React rewrite of our legacy WinForms tool — roughly the same arc QuickBooks went through moving from desktop to web. We achieved about 80% feature parity at launch. I owned quality across the rebuild: hundreds of bugs identified and tracked through resolution, release notes compiled, and handover sessions run with sales, marketing, professional services, and support before we cut over.

In parallel I led our move from Azure DevOps to Linear. The hard part wasn’t the tooling switch — it was treating the migration as an excuse to clean house. We collapsed a five-tier hierarchy (Epic → Feature → User Story → Bug/Task) into Linear’s three (Initiatives → Projects → Issues), pruned a backlog that had accumulated years of “someday” items, and rebuilt what remained around what we’d actually deliver in the next two quarters.

Climbing

I worked at Movement (formerly The Cliffs) for nearly ten years before this career, and I still climb a few times a week. Climbing trains basically every muscle and pays dividends in everything else — opening jars, carrying groceries, the occasional piece of furniture you underestimated. It’s also unusually social for an athletic pursuit; everyone in the gym wants you to succeed, and I’ve met most of my closest friends at the wall. I prefer indoor bouldering — a mild fear of heights makes rope climbing less appealing — but I’ve spent plenty of weekends on rock outside New Paltz, where I went to school.

Outside work

I’ve always loved physics, and outer space is where the most incredible displays of physics happen. What draws me to astronomy is that you can point at a smudge of light from across the galaxy and pull real information out of it — age, distance, composition. With a diffraction grating filter on my Celestron telescope and some open-source software, I identified the elements present in the Ring Nebula (the remains of a sun-like star) and assigned a color to each. I take the scope out a few times a year — camping, friends’ backyards, the occasional Airbnb in the middle of nowhere — but in Charlottesville I mostly use the campus observatory, which has much more aperture than mine and pulls in far more detail. The moon, Jupiter, and Saturn never get old.

I cook ambitiously and treat the kitchen like a chemistry lab. There’s a lot of fermentation involved — sourdough, yogurt, kimchi, pickles of basically any vegetable, garlic honey — and a lot of “tried it at a restaurant, want to make it at home”: sushi, curry, garlic chili oil, bread, elaborate salads. My family taught me the basics; AI and YouTube fill in the rest. The only thing that ever stops me is a missing piece of equipment.

I hike — sometimes traveling for it, sometimes just local trails with my dog. Russian is my first language but I only speak it; I use it mostly with family. English is my working language for everything else.
